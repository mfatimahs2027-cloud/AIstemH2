Slide 1: Big Cats

Core Big Cats (The Classic 5)

  • Lion
  • Tiger
  • Leopard
  • Jaguar
  • Snow Leopard

Additional Large Wild Cats Often Included

  • Cheetah — super fast runner
  • Cougar (Mountain Lion / Puma) — sneaky and adaptable
  • Clouded Leopard — great climber with big fangs
  • Sunda Clouded Leopard — similar but found in Borneo/Sumatra

The strict scientific count is 5 big cat species (the roaring Panthera group), but many people talk about 7 or 8 when including cheetahs, cougars, and clouded leopards.

The entire cat family (Felidae) has about 40–41 wild cat species total — most are smaller cats like bobcats, ocelots, servals, and lynx.

Personality Traits of the Big Cats

Lion — The confident team leader

Lions are social, protective, and proud. They live in groups (prides), cooperate in hunting, and roar loudly to show dominance.

Tiger — The strong, independent lone warrior

Tigers are solitary, powerful, and serious. They prefer independence and are calm but fierce hunters.

Leopard — The clever, sneaky ninja

Leopards are shy, adaptable, and excellent climbers. They survive almost anywhere and hunt quietly at night.

Jaguar — The bold, powerful powerhouse

Jaguars are fearless, muscular, and love water. They hunt with incredible strength and confidence.

Snow Leopard — The quiet, shy mountain ghost

Snow leopards are gentle, elusive, and peaceful. They live in cold mountains and move silently.

Cheetah — The speedy, nervous sprinter

Cheetahs are fast but timid. They rely on speed, not strength, and often feel anxious around stronger predators.

Cougar — The stealthy, quiet explorer

Cougars are secretive, calm, and adaptable. They travel long distances alone and hunt silently.

Slide 2: The G.O.A.T

Goats are curious, intelligent animals found in both domestic and wild forms. They belong to the Bovidae family and are known for being playful, smart, and amazing climbers.

Domestic Goats (Capra hircus)

Domestic goats were one of the first animals ever domesticated by humans, around 10,000 years ago in the Middle East.

  • There are 200–300+ breeds worldwide.
  • Raised for milk, meat, fiber, and companionship.
  • Goats are intelligent, social, and clean eaters.
  • They have rectangular pupils for wide-angle vision.
  • Baby goats = kids, females = does/nannies, males = bucks.
  • Gestation is about 5 months, and twins are common.

Domestic goats are playful, curious, affectionate, and sometimes stubborn — but always fun.

Wild Goats

Wild goats live in rocky mountains and are extremely agile and tough. Domestic goats mostly descend from the wild goat (Capra aegagrus).

  • Ibex — Alpine, Nubian, Siberian.
  • Markhor — large wild goat with spiral horns.
  • Tur — Caucasian mountain goats.
  • Walia ibex — rare species from Ethiopia.

Fun Fact

The markhor, the national animal of Pakistan, has incredible corkscrew-shaped horns that can grow up to 5 feet long.

Slide 3: Sharks

Sharks are ancient ocean animals that have existed for over 450 million years. They are fish, not mammals, and many are apex predators that keep ocean ecosystems healthy.

How Many Species Are There?

There are over 500 species of sharks, with new ones still being discovered. Most live in saltwater, but a few can enter freshwater.

Where Do Sharks Live?

  • Shallow coral reefs
  • Open ocean
  • Deep sea
  • Cold polar waters
  • Warm tropical seas

Fun Shark Facts

  • Sharks have no bones — only cartilage.
  • They have super senses for smell, vibration, and electricity.
  • They can grow thousands of teeth in a lifetime.
  • Some must swim to breathe; others can rest.
  • Baby sharks are called pups.
  • A group of sharks is called a shiver.

Some Famous Shark Types

Whale Shark

The largest fish in the world — gentle plankton eaters.

Great White Shark

Strong hunters, often just curious about humans.

Shortfin Mako Shark

The fastest shark, reaching 45+ mph.

Hammerhead Shark

Hammer-shaped head improves vision and hunting.

Tiger Shark

Known for eating almost anything.

Dwarf Lanternshark

The smallest shark, only 8 inches long.

Greenland Shark

Lives for hundreds of years in icy waters.

Basking Shark

Second-largest shark, filters plankton.

Important Note

Many shark species are endangered due to overfishing. Despite their reputation, sharks are not very dangerous to humans.
